How Long Does It Take to Break Even on a Refinance?

May 15, 2026 · 5 min read · Calculate yours in 60 seconds →

The national average break-even period is about 23 months — but that number is meaningless for your specific situation. Your break-even depends on 4 variables unique to you. Here's how to find yours.

What "Break-Even" Actually Means

When you refinance, you pay closing costs upfront — typically $5,000–$9,000. In return, you get a lower monthly payment. The break-even point is the month when your cumulative monthly savings finally equal what you paid in closing costs.

Before the break-even month: refinancing has cost you money. After it: every month is pure profit.

Break-even (months) = Closing Costs ÷ Monthly Savings

Example A
$6,000 costs ÷ $250/mo savings
= 24 months ✓
Example B
$8,000 costs ÷ $180/mo savings
= 44 months ~
Example C
$5,500 costs ÷ $350/mo savings
= 16 months ✓✓

The 4 Variables That Determine Your Break-Even

1. Your closing costs (biggest variable)

This is the numerator in the formula — and it varies more than most people expect. Two homeowners with identical loans in different states can have closing costs that differ by $3,000–$5,000. States that require attorneys at closing (New York, Florida, Georgia, and others) or that charge mortgage recording taxes will push your costs — and your break-even — significantly higher.

2. Your rate reduction

A larger rate drop means larger monthly savings, which shrinks your break-even period. But rate drops interact with your loan balance — a 0.75% rate reduction on a $450,000 loan saves far more per month than the same reduction on a $180,000 balance. This is why the old "2% rule" doesn't hold: the loan size matters just as much as the rate change.

3. Your remaining loan balance

Higher balances generate more dollar savings from the same rate reduction. A homeowner with $400,000 remaining typically breaks even in fewer months than one with $150,000 remaining — even with identical rate drops and closing costs — because the absolute monthly savings are larger.

4. Your new loan term

If you refinance from 22 years remaining into a new 30-year loan, your monthly payment drops significantly — but some of that "savings" is just extending your payoff date, not actually saving you money. A shorter new term (15 or 20 years) usually means less monthly savings but dramatically less interest over the life of the loan. The break-even calculator accounts for this.

The One Thing That Can Make a Long Break-Even OK

How certain are you that you'll stay in the home? A 48-month break-even is uncomfortable but acceptable if you just bought the house last year and have no plans to move. The same 48-month break-even is a bad deal if you're already thinking about upsizing, relocating for work, or approaching retirement and considering downsizing.

Life changes are hard to predict. Most financial planners suggest using a conservative estimate — if you think you might move in 5 years, plan as if it's 4. A break-even that requires you to stay perfectly on schedule is fragile.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is a 3-year (36-month) break-even worth it?

Yes — if you're confident you'll stay in the home for at least 5 years. You'd net 2 full years (24 months) of savings after recovering closing costs, which on a $200–$300/month savings rate is $4,800–$7,200 in your pocket.

What if my break-even is longer than I'll stay?

Don't refinance. You'd pay closing costs but leave before recovering them — a net loss. The exception is a no-closing-cost refinance where the lender covers fees in exchange for a slightly higher rate; in that case the "break-even" is month 1.

Does the break-even calculation change for a cash-out refinance?

Yes — significantly. A cash-out refinance increases your loan balance, which changes both the monthly payment and the interest cost calculation. The standard break-even formula doesn't fully capture this. Use a dedicated cash-out calculator for those scenarios.

Should I include tax deductions in my break-even?

Only if you itemize deductions. Since the 2018 tax reform raised the standard deduction, fewer than 15% of taxpayers itemize. If you don't itemize, mortgage interest deductions don't affect your break-even math at all.
