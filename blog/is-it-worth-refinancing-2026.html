Is It Worth Refinancing in 2026? The Break-Even Rule Explained

May 25, 2026 · 8 min read · Use the calculator →

The short answer: Refinancing is worth it if your break-even point — the month your savings exceed your closing costs — happens before you plan to sell or move. Here's exactly how to calculate that number.

The Problem With the "2% Rule"

For decades, the standard advice was: only refinance if you can lower your interest rate by at least 2%. On a 30-year mortgage in the 1990s, that made sense — loan balances were smaller, closing costs were a higher percentage of savings, and most homeowners stayed in their homes for 20+ years.

Today, none of those conditions hold universally. The average US mortgage balance is now well above $300,000. Closing costs have risen. And the median time homeowners stay in a home before selling is just 8–10 years. A rigid 2% threshold misses good refinance opportunities and approves bad ones.

The right question isn't "how much does my rate drop?" It's "when do I break even, and how long will I be here?"

The Break-Even Formula

The math is straightforward:

Break-even (months) = Closing Costs ÷ Monthly Savings

If you stay past the break-even month, refinancing profits you money.

Example: Your closing costs are $7,200 and your new payment is $210/month lower. Your break-even point is 7,200 ÷ 210 = 34 months (about 2 years and 10 months). If you plan to stay 5+ years, refinancing nets you $5,400 in pure savings after recovering the closing costs.

When the Break-Even Rule Has Exceptions

1. You're resetting to 30 years

If you're 8 years into a 30-year mortgage and you refinance into a new 30-year loan, your break-even calculation doesn't capture the full picture. Yes, your monthly payment drops. But you've also extended your payoff date by 8 years — meaning significantly more total interest paid over the life of the loan. Consider refinancing into a 20 or 22-year term to keep your payoff date roughly the same.

2. No-closing-cost refinancing

Some lenders offer to waive or roll closing costs into the loan in exchange for a slightly higher interest rate. This can make sense if: you have limited cash, your break-even would be very long, or you plan to refinance again in a few years. The trade-off is a permanently higher rate. Use the calculator to compare both scenarios.

3. Cash-out refinancing

If you're pulling equity out of your home when you refinance, the break-even calculation changes significantly. Your closing costs are higher, your new balance is larger, and the rate is typically 0.25–0.5% higher than a standard rate-and-term refinance. A separate cash-out calculator is the right tool for that scenario.

What Rates Look Like Right Now

As of late May 2026, average refinance rates are:

  • 30-year fixed refinance: 6.76%
  • 15-year fixed refinance: 6.09%
  • VA IRRRL (veterans): ~5.75%

Homeowners who locked in rates of 7.5%+ during the 2023–2024 rate peaks are the strongest candidates for refinancing today. Refinance application volume was up 62% year-over-year as of April 2026 (Fannie Mae), suggesting many homeowners are already running these numbers.

The Bottom Line

Refinancing is worth it when your break-even month comes before you plan to sell — with enough runway after that point to make a meaningful difference. A break-even of 24 months with a 7-year time horizon is excellent. A break-even of 48 months with a 3-year move plan is a loss.

The 2% rule is a starting point, not the answer. Your exact numbers are the answer.
