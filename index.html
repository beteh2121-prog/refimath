How Does Mortgage Refinance Break-Even Work?

Refinancing replaces your current mortgage with a new one at a different rate. The catch: you pay closing costs upfront — typically 2–3% of your loan balance. The break-even point is the month when your cumulative monthly savings finally equal those upfront costs.

The formula is simple: Break-even (months) = Closing costs ÷ Monthly savings. If you plan to stay in your home longer than the break-even period, refinancing is likely worthwhile.

The "2% Rule" — and Why It's Outdated

You may have heard the rule "only refinance if you can lower your rate by 2%." That was reasonable advice decades ago when closing costs were lower and loan balances were smaller. Today, with the average home loan above $300,000, even a 0.5% rate reduction can justify refinancing if your closing costs are reasonable and you plan to stay long enough.

What Closing Costs Are You Paying?

Closing costs typically include:

  • Loan origination fee (0.5–1% of loan balance)
  • Appraisal fee ($400–$700)
  • Title insurance and search ($500–$1,500)
  • Recording fees ($25–$250, varies by state)
  • Prepaid interest, taxes, and insurance (varies)
  • Attorney fees (required in some states)

On a $320,000 loan, expect $6,400–$9,600 in total closing costs. Some lenders offer "no-closing-cost" refinancing, but the costs are typically rolled into a higher interest rate.

When Refinancing Doesn't Make Sense

Even a low break-even point doesn't guarantee refinancing is right for you. Consider skipping a refinance if: you plan to sell or move within 2–3 years, you're deep into your loan term (resetting to 30 years costs more in lifetime interest), your credit score has dropped significantly, or you're planning to cash out equity (different calculation applies).

Frequently Asked Questions

How long does it take to break even on a refinance?
Most homeowners break even on a refinance within 18–36 months. The exact timeline depends on your closing costs, the size of your rate reduction, and your remaining loan balance. Use the calculator above to get your precise break-even month.
Is it worth refinancing to save $200 a month?
Possibly. If you're saving $200/month and your closing costs are $6,000, your break-even is 30 months. If you plan to stay in the home for 5+ years, that's $12,000+ in net savings — likely worth it. Run the full numbers in the calculator.
What is a good break-even point for refinancing?
A break-even of under 24 months is generally considered excellent. Under 36 months is good for most homeowners. Anything over 48–60 months is risky unless you're very confident you'll stay in the home long-term.
Does refinancing reset my mortgage to 30 years?
Only if you choose a 30-year term. You can refinance into a 15, 20, or 25-year mortgage to pay off your home faster while still lowering your rate. A shorter term usually means a lower rate but a higher monthly payment.
Should I refinance if I plan to sell in 3 years?
It depends on your break-even point. If you can break even in under 18 months and you're staying 3 years, you'd net 18 months of pure savings. If your break-even is 30 months, you'd barely cover the closing costs before selling. Use the calculator with "years to stay" set to 3 to see your net position.
